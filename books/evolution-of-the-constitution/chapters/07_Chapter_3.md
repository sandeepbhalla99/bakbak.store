# Chapter 3

The Working of the Indian Constitution

3.1. To THE QUESTION as to what is the form of Government established by the Constitution, the answer given in the last lecture was that leaving emergency measures, it is, in essence federal. That  conclusion was reached on an es: the Constitution bearing on the question. But that is only one aspect of the matter. There is another aspect- and possibly that is more important and that is the working of the Constitution.

"A country may have a federal Constitution but in practice it may work that Constitution in such a way that its Government is not federal. Or, a country with a non-federal Constitution may work it in such a way that it provides an example of federal Government."[	 Wheare, Federal Government, 4th Edition, Pg. 385.]

The learned author gives Canada as an instance of the latter. Under the British North America Act, 1867, the Dominion Government can veto a Bill passed by the Provincial Legislature. It can require the Lieutenant Governor of the Province to reserve any such Bill for consideration by itself. These are undoubtedly unitary features. But in practice, these powers were seldom exercised by the Dominion government with the result that even though the Constitution is on its terms unfederal, in its actual working, it is federal. A discussion, therefor federal character of the Indian Constitution will be incomplete without an examination of its actual working.

3.2. At the very outset, it deserves to be emphasised that in the working of the Constitution ever since it came into force on 26th January 1950, India has shown herself both able and willing to shoulder the responsibilities of a democratic federal Government. The electorate has become increasingly political-minded, as witness the interest taken in the recent elections to the legislatures. The Parliamentary form of government is well enthroned in the government of the Union and of the States and there is full realisation by the Ministers that their actions are under scrutiny by a critical public. The judiciary has, by its independence and ability, inspired confidence in the minds of the public, that it will secure for all citizens justice, liberty, equality and fraternity as envisaged in the preamble to the Constitution. One has only to look around and see how many democratic federal Governments established in recent times have broken down under the stress of dictatorship of the military and of the proletariat. We have therefore every reason to be satisfied with our achievements so far. But very much more remains to be done. There are trends, which, if not curbed, might undermine the very foundations of the democratic federal structure of our Constitution. We must be vigilant and on the watch for such forces and eliminate them. What these forces are, we must now consider.

3.3. PartXVIII of the Constitution enacts what are called Emergency provisions. Article 352 provides that 'when India is threatened by war or external aggression or armed rebellion[	Substituted for 'internal disturbance' by Constitution (44th) Amendment Act.], the President might issue a Proclamation of Emergency. Article 353 enacts that while the Emergency is in operation the Union Executive can give directions to the States, and the Union Legislature can enact laws on subjects within the exclusive competence of the States. Under Article 354, the President can during the period of Emergency, suspend the operation of Articles 268 to 279, which provide for the distribution of certain items of Revenue to the States. Article 358 provides that when a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation, the State can enact a law unfettered by the provisions of Article 19, and Article 359 empowers the President to suspend, during the period of Emergency, the right to enforce the Fundamental rights under PartIII.

3.4. There can be no doubt that these powers are wholly totalitarian in their contents and inconsistent with the fundamental principles of federation. On the question whether by reason of these provisions, the Constitution should be classified as unitarian, it has already been stated that being emergency provisions, they cannot be taken into account in considering the true character of the Constitution in its normal working. But then, for that very reason, action should be taken under this article only when there is a real emergency and it should cease to operate when the emergency ceases. We had such an emergency when the Chinese invaded India in 1962. A Proclamation of Emergency was issued under this Article and there is no dispute that it was rightly done.

3.5.1. But then, Chinese war came to a stop but the Emergency Proclamation was not withdrawn and it is this that has come in for considerable criticism. It has been said that as the Fundamental rights of the citizens are under eclipse while a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation, its continuance after the emergency ceases would be unconstitutional and that in fact it was being used for purposes not within the intent of the Article. " The arbitrary and exclusive powers," said the ex-Attorney-General Shri M. C. Setalvad, " assumed under the Defence of India Act and the Rules have been exercised for the ordinary purpose of Government, to prevent traders from hoarding commodities, to put down strikes and for a variety of normal functions of Government which could and should be dealt with under powers conferred by ordinary law of the land." This forcibly brings out how a Constitution which is federal in its provisions can be unitary in its working. In theory, a provision such as Article 352 is necessary even for a federal Constitution, but in practice, it will be unfederal to use it for purposes not within its contemplation, for regulating the rights of citizens which should be done under the ordinary law of the land and subject to all the safeguards provided by the Constitution.

3.5.2. As apprehended the provision of section 352 was again abused and on June 26th 1975 a proclamation of Emergency was made on the ground of internal disturbances. Fundamental rights and habeas corpus was also suspended. The situation is summed up by Fazal Ali J. in these words:

"It would thus appear that in the nine states referred to above, the Congress party was practically routed. It is also clear that the voters who voted for the candidates standing for the Lok Sabha in the States were more or less the same who had voted the Congress party in the State Assemblies during the previous elections. Thus, summarising the position in short, it is clear

(1) that a grave emergency was clamped in the whole country;

(2) that civil liberties were withdrawn to a great extent;

(3) that important fundamental rights of the people were suspended;

(4) that strict censorship on the press was placed; and

(5) that the judicial powers were crippled to a large extent.

In the new elections the Congress party suffered a major reverse in the nine states and the people displayed complete lack of confidence in the Congress party. The cumulative effect of the circumstances mentioned above may lead to a reasonable inference that the people had given a massive verdict not only against the Congress candidates who fought the elections to the Lok Sabha but also to the policies and ideologies followed by the Congress Governments as a whole whether at the Centre or in the States during the twenty months preceding the elections."[	State of Rajsthan v. Union of India, 1978 (1) SCR 1 at p. 118]

Bhagwati J., summed up the position of the ruling party after it's defeat in the Centre, in these words:

"There has been a total rout of candidates belonging to the ruling party. In some of the Plaintiff States, the ruling party has not been able to secure a single seat. Never in the history of this country has such a clear and unequivocal verdict being given by the people, never a more massive vote of no-confidence in the ruling party. When there is such crushing defeat suffered by the ruling party and the people have expressed themselves categorically against its policies, it is symptomatic of complete alienation between the Government and the people. It is axiomatic that no Government can function efficiently and effectively in accordance with the Constitution in a democratic set up unless it enjoys the goodwill and support of the people. Where there is a wall of estrangement which divides the Government from the people, and there is resentment and antipathy in the hearts of the people against the Government, it is not at all unlikely that it may lead to instability and even the administration may be paralysed. The consent of the people is the basis of democratic form of Government and when that is withdrawn so entirely and un- equivocally as to leave no room for doubt about the intensity of public feeling against the ruling party, the moral authority of the Government would be seriously undermined and a situation may arise where the people may cease to give respect and obedience to governmental authority and even conflict and confrontation may develop between the Government and the people leading to collapse of administration."[	Ibid at p. 85.]

3.5.2. In view of this gross abuse of power, the Constitution by its 44th Amendment has made a variety of changes in the constitution to ensure a check on this drastic powers and to ensure that these powers are exercised only in case of real emergency and not to serve vested interest of the majority in the house.

3.5.3. Article 352, relating to Proclamation of Emergency, has been amended in several respects. In the first place, the ground of "internal disturbance" had been substituted by the ground of "armed rebellion". In the second place, it has been provided that the President will not issue a Proclamation of Emergency unless the decision of the Union Cabinet that such a Proclamation may be issued has been communicated to him in writing . In the third place, it has been provided that a Proclamation of Emergency will has to be approved within a period of one month (instead of two months) by resolutions of both Houses of Parliament and that such resolutions Will have to be passed by a majority of total membership of each House and by a majority of not less than two-third of the members present and voting in each House (instead of a simple majority), In the fourth place, it has been provided that for the continuance of the Proclamation of emergency, approval by resolutions of both Houses will be required every six months. In the fifth place, it has been provided that a Proclamation of emergency will be revoked whenever the House of the People passes a resolution (by a simple majority) disapproving its continuance. It has also been provided that one-tenth of the total membership of the House of the People may, by notice (to the Speaker, if the House is in session, or, to the President, if the House is not in session), requisition a special sitting of the House for considering the continuance of a Proclamation of Emergency. The provisions in regard to revocation of a Proclamation of Emergency pursuant to a resolution by the House of the People to that effect and holding of a special sitting of the house for considering the question of continuance of a Proclamation of Emergency will apply in relation to a proclamation varying such Proclamation as well. Clause (5) of article 352, making the satisfaction of the President as to existence of a grave emergency necessitating the issue of a Proclamation of Emergency final, has been omitted.

3.5.4. Article 358, relating to the suspension of the provisions of article 19 (regarding citizens' freedoms of speech, assembly, movement, residence and settlement, association and occupation) during emergencies, has been amended. Under the article as amended, the provisions of article 19 will become suspended only in the case of a Proclamation of Emergency issued on the ground of war or external aggression and not in the case of a Proclamation of Emergency issued on the ground of armed rebellion. Further, the suspension of article 19 under article 358 will not apply in relation to any law which does not contain a recital to the effect that such law is being made in relation to the Proclamation of Emergency or to any executive action taken otherwise than under a law

3.5.5. Similarly Article 350. relating to suspension of the enforcement of the rights conferred by PartIII of the Constitution during Emergencies, has been amended in two respects. The first amendment is for providing that the enforcement of rights under article 20 (relating to protection in respect of conviction for offences) and article 21 (relating to protection of life and personal liberty) cannot be suspended. The second amendment is for providing that the suspension of enforcement of any right under the article will not apply in relation to any law which does not contain a recital to the effect that such law is being made in relation to the Proclamation of Emergency in operation or to any executive action taken otherwise than under a law containing such a recital.

3.6.1. Then there is Article 356. It provides that if the President is satisfied that the Government of the States cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution, he can issue a Proclamation of Emergency and when that is done, the executive power of the State becomes vested in the Union and the legislative power in the Union Parliament. It cannot be doubted that there should be power in the Union Government to take over the administration of the State when there is a breakdown of the State Government. The controversy is as to what the Article means by the words "when the government of the State cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution."

3.6.2. There have been several instances in which the powers under this Article have been exercised and President's rule established. They are, mostly, occasions when the Ministry in power had lost the confidence of the legislature and it was not possible to form another Ministry which could command a majority in the House. There was also one instance, when the Congress Ministry in Andhra, which had a majority in the legislature declined to carry on the administration owing to internal disputes and then action was taken under this Article. In all these cases, the Union Government has acted. on the view that if it is not possible to form a stable Ministry in a State, then that is a breakdown of the government calling for intervention under Article 356. It has been said in opposition to this view that according to its true intent, this Article is applicable only when " there is (1) a breakdown of law and order within a State, or (2) widespread confusion during general elections or (3) organised terrorism or intimidation of the civil authorities ; that when a motion of no-confidence is passed against the Ministry, the proper course to be adopted would be to give an opportunity for any other party or parties to form a Government and that if that is not possible, to dissolve the legislature and order fresh elections : and that to exercise the powers under this Article on such occasions, would be unconstitutional."[	Santhanam, Prof. J. Franco Lectures. 1965-66 page 5.] Without attempting to define exhaustively the circumstances under which action could be taken under Article 356, it is sufficient to say that if there is difficulty in the formation of a stable Ministry, the parliamentary convention is to order a fresh election. Article 356 should be invoked only when the machinery of government cannot be worked under the ordinary law.

3.6.3. Since the ruling party at the Centre, often tend to be partisan in taking the decision, the Supreme Court has now held that the decision under article 356 shall be amenable to judicial review.[	S.R. Bommai vs Union of India] As to what shall be the scope of review excluding the political question if any, is to be evolved.

3.6.4. A few examples of precedents from America would be apt in support of this provision. American Constitution has no provision similar to those referred to above. On various occasions the local Governments acted contrary to the directions of Federal Government which was forced to take direct actions. The position of federal Government is summed up by US Supreme Court in these words:

"As, under the Constitution, power over interstate commerce and the transportation of the mails is vested in the national government, and Congress, by virtue of such grant, has assumed actual and direct control, it follows that the national government may prevent any unlawful and forcible interference therewith. But how shall this be accomplished? Doubtless it is within the competency of Congress to prescribe by legislation that any interference with these matters shall be offences against the United States, and prosecuted and punished by indictment in the proper courts. But is that the only remedy? Have the vast interests of the nation in interstate commerce and in the transportation of the mails no other protection than lies in the possible punishment of those who interfere with it? To ask the question is to answer it. By article 3, section 2, clause 3, of the Federal Constitution, it is provided:

'The trial of all crimes except in cases of impeachment shall be by jury; and such trial shall be held in the State where the said crime shall have been committed.'

If all the inhabitants of a State, or even a great body of them, should combine to obstruct interstate commerce or the transportation of the mails, prosecutions for such offences had in such a community would be doomed in advance to failure. And if the certainty of such failure was known, and the national government had no other way to enforce the freedom of interstate commerce and the transportation of the mails than by prosecution and punishment for interference therewith, the whole interests of the nation in these respects would be at the absolute mercy of a portion of the inhabitants of that single State.

But there is no such impotency in the national government. The entire strength of the nation may be used to enforce in any part of the land the full and free exercise of all national powers and the security of all rights entrusted by the Constitution to its care. The strong arm of the national government may be put forth to brush away all obstructions to the freedom of interstate commerce or the transportation of the mails. If the emergency arises, the army of the Nation, and all its militia, are at the service of the Nation to compel obedience to its laws."[	In re: Debs, 158 US 564.]

3.6.5. Under Article 4, of US Constitution it is the duty of the Federal Government to ensure that the States shall have republic form of Government. Without the above 'strength of nation' would it be possible for Federal Government to ensure that the States would follow the republican principle?  There are many examples of Federal Government of USA using force to ensure that that the States may respect and obey Federal law and writs. The US Supreme Court has observed in this regard as under:

"........ the Constitution of the United States, as far as it has provided for an emergency of this kind and authorized the general government to interfere in the domestic concerns of a State, has treated the subject as political in its nature, and placed the power in the hands of that department.

The fourth section of the fourth article of the Constitution of the United States provides that the United States shall guarantee to every State in the Union a republican form of government, and shall protect each of them against invasion, and on the application of the legislature or of the executive (when the legislature cannot be convened) against domestic violence.

Under this article of the Constitution, it rests with Congress to decide what government is the established one in a State. For as the United States guarantee to each State a republican government, Congress must necessarily decide what government is established in the State before it can determine whether it is republican or not. And when the senators and representatives of a State are admitted into the councils of the Union, the authority of the government under which they are appointed, as well as its republican character, is recognized by the proper constitutional authority. And its decision is binding on every other department of the government, and could not be questioned in a judicial tribunal. It is true that the contest in this case did not last long enough to bring the matter to this issue, and, as no senators or representatives were elected under the authority of the government of which Mr. Dorr was the head, Congress was not called upon to decide the controversy. Yet the right to decide is placed there, and not in the courts."[	Luther v. Borden, 48 US 1.]

For those interested in instances of direct action by Federal Government in USA may find the incident of Little Rock Nine in Arkansas interesting where USA had to use armed forces to enforce the orders of US Supreme Court. A similar action was also taken in respect of Mississippi in 1962 called as Ole Miss riot of 1962, in which US Martial were employed to get a Black student admitted in Mississippi University.[	Pictures (courtesy Wikipedia) on next pages.] Section 119 of the Australian Constitution is also in similar to the terms of Article 4 of US Constitution. It is apparent, the provisions in the constitution of India merely recognises de jure what is de facto practiced in other Constitutional Federations. Thus a power essential for existence of federal govt. can not be said to impair the federal principle itself.[	Seervai, H.M. In Constitutional Law of India, Fourth Edition p. 293.]

3.6.6. By 44th Amendment of the Constitution of India the Article 356, relating to the President's power to issue a Proclamation in case of failure of constitutional machinery in a State, has been amended. Under the article as amended, such a Proclamation will, upon its being approved first by resolutions by both Houses of Parliament, continue for Six months from the date of issue of Proclamation (instead of one year from the date of the second of the resolutions approving the proclamation) and upon its being approval likewise on a subsequent occasion, it will continue for a further period of six months (instead of one year). However, a resolution with respect to the continuance in force of a Proclamation under the article for any period beyond the expiration of one year from the date of issue of such Proclamation shall not be passed by either House of Parliament unless a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation at the time of the passing of such resolution and Election Commission certifies that the continuance in force of the Proclamation during the period specified in such resolution is necessary on account of difficulties in holding elections to the Legislative Assembly of the State concerned.

3.7. Then there is Article 360 which authorises the President to issue a Proclamation of Emergency when there is a financial crisis. By 44th Amendment the Article 360, relating to financial emergency, has been amended. The first amendment is for the purpose of making the article self-contained in view of the provisions of article 352 incorporated by reference in article 360 having been amended. The second amendment is for omitting clause (5) of article 360 which makes the satisfaction of the President as to the arising of a situation whereby the financial stability or credit of the country or any part thereof is threatened final and conclusive. Thus his decision may be justiciable. It cannot be said that there is no need for such a provision. No occasion would appear to have arisen for invoking the power under this article and so there is no need to consider it. But there are other provisions in the Constitution dealing with the financial relations between the Union Government and the States and action taken under them has been criticised as unfederal. This must now be examined.

3.8. To understand the somewhat complicated provisions regulating the financial relationship between the Union and the States, it is necessary to know something of the working of the system prior to the Constitution. It is sufficient if we start with the Government of India Act, 1919. Under that Act, the several heads of revenue were divided between the Centre and the Provinces ; income-tax and excise other than liquor being exclusively allotted to the Centre, and land revenue, liquor excise and stamps to the Provinces. This rigid division of the sources of revenue between the Centre and the Provinces did not work satisfactorily. So the Government of India Act, 1935 introduced a modification. While it distributed the several heads of taxes between the Centre, and the Provinces respectively under entries 54 to 58 in List I, and entries 39 to 53 in List II, it also provided for assignment of a portion of the revenue realised by the Central Government under certain heads to the Provinces. Section 138 provided that a prescribed percentage of the receipts of the Central Government under income-tax should be assigned to the States and under Section 140, a share in the tax receipts on sale, federal excise and export duties might be given to the States if the Federal legislature so enacted. The constitution has in general adopted this scheme and elaborated it.

3.9.1. Pausing here, one must note a fundamental difference in the exercise of the power of taxation in America and in India. In America, there is no division of the heads of taxation between the Congress and the States. They are sovereign each in its domain and can raise revenue by tax on all sources, according to their own requirements. Thus, it comes about that the same person is subject to double taxation on the same head of income, one by the Congress, and the other by the State Government under their respective laws. Apart from the hardship to the assessee, this dual power of taxation has given rise to certain problems which it is unnecessary to consider here. But in India, there is only one field of taxation, the several heads thereof being divided between the Union and the States. One result of this is that adjustments have to be made in the distribution of the total receipts, so that the States might be self-sufficient and be able to carry on their undertakings.

3.9.2. At the same time there is, however, fundamental similarity in Indian Parliament's power of taxation and American Congress. The power to levy tax is absolute except to the extent when it is either confiscatory or is a colourable piece of legislation aimed but the taxation cannot be used to curtail the freedom of press. It was observed by US Supreme court:

"The tax imposed is designated a "license tax for the privilege of engaging in such business" -- that is to say, the business of selling, or making any charge for, advertising. As applied to appellees, it is a tax of two percent. on the gross receipts derived from advertisements carried in their newspapers when, and only when, the newspapers of each enjoy a circulation of more than 20,000 copies per week. It thus operates as a restraint in a double sense. First, its effect is to curtail the amount of revenue realized from advertising, and, second, its direct tendency is to restrict circulation. This is plain enough when we consider that, if it were increased to a high degree, as it could be if valid, it well might result in destroying both advertising and circulation."[	Grosjean v. American Press Co., (1973) 297 US 233.]

The above decision has been followed by Supreme Court of India with approval in Express Newspaper Case[	Express Newspapers (P) Ltd. v. Union of India, 1959 SCR 12: AIR 1958 SC 578.] Thus Indian law is also same that a tax law or a quantum of tax cannot be challenged on the ground that rate of tax is very high. [	Jagannath v.. Union Of India 1962 (2) SCR 118: AIR 1962 SC 148.] Unless it is colourable exercise of power.[	K.T. Moopil Nair v. State of Kerela 1961(3) SCR 77 : AIR 1961 SC 552.]

3.10. The scheme of taxation adopted by the Constitution may now be summarised. The several items of taxes which can be levied by the States are set out under entries 82 to 92-A in List I. Some of them belong exclusively to the Union Government as for example, Customs under entry 83, Corporation tax under entry 85, tax on capital value, entry 86. As for the others, though they are all of them levied by the Union Legislature, they differ among themselves as regards the incident of their collection and distribution. first, under Article 268 stamp duties and duties on medicine and toilet preparations mentioned therein are to be collected by the States in which they are levied and the proceeds also taken by them. Secondly, there are certain taxes which are to be collected by the Union Government and distributed among the States in such manner as may be determined by Parliament. They are set out in Article 269. Thirdly, there is income-tax which has to be apportioned between the Union and the several States in such manner as the President might on the recommendation of the finance Commission decide. That is provided in Article 270. And lastly, there is Article 272 under which it is left to the Parliament to decide whether certain excise duties mentioned therein should be shared with the States and if so in what proportion. Then, there is Article 275 which also has a bearing on this question though it is not one imposing a tax. It provides for grants being given by the Union to States which might be in need of assistance. The finance Commission has to recommend the principles on which the grants are to be made to them. Under this Article different sums may be fixed for different States.

3.11. The above is a resume of. the provisions governing the distribution of revenue between the Union and the States. They follow in general the lines laid down in the Government of India Act, 1935 and are more elaborate and comprehensive. On the face of the provisions aforesaid, it cannot be said that there is anything in them inconsistent with the federal character of the relations between the Union and the States.

3.12. There is one feature in this system which requires to be specially noted. It is the Constitution of a finance Commission, under Article 280. That Article provides that finance Commission should be appointed within two years of the Constitution and thereafter again once in five years. The duties of the Commission are inter alia to recommend the mode of division of receipts of income-tax under Article 270 and the principles on which grants-in-aid should be given to the States under Article 275. It is a high-powered body and its record has been impressive and such as to inspire confidence among the States. No exception can be taken to the need and utility of such a body.

3.13. But then, there is another body which exerts enormous influence in the sphere of finance and it is this that has come in for considerable criticism. It is the Planning Commission. This body was set up by the Union Government in 1950. It has no statutory authority. Its constitution is what the Union Government for the time being may decide. The Prime Minister is its chairman and among its members are the Planning Minister, the finance Minister and some other Ministers and a few non-officials. In this set up, while, on the one hand, the Planning Commission would act on the advice of the Union Government, the latter would on the other hand accept the decisions of the former. Thus the Planning Commission is practically a limb of. the Union Government. As the plans have, most of them, to be worked in the States and through their agency, with a view to ensure their cooperation, the Planning Commission has evolved a machinery and established a body called The National Development Council. Its members are the Cabinet Members of the Union Government, Members of the Planning Commission and the Chief Ministers of all the States. It is, of course, not a statutory body but plays an important role in the work of the Planning Commission, and its decisions are as a matter of convention accepted as binding by the Union Government, and the States. "The position of the National Development Council", says Shri Santhanam, " has come to approximate to that of a super-Cabinet over the entire Indian Federation, a Cabinet functioning for the Government of India and the Governments of all the States."[	 Union and State Relations, page 47.]

3.14. The purpose for which the Planning Commission has been constituted is to formulate schemes for national development and in practice its activities have extended over a large variety of topics—Industry, Agriculture, Education, Arts and Science and Social Welfare. Having regard to the very wide and unbounded area over which the Commission operates and the huge financial commitments involved therein, it is not a matter for surprise that the Planning Commission has come to dominate the field of finance and that has naturally provoked considerable criticism about its Constitutionality and work. It has been said that the planning for the purposes of economic development has become all comprehensive and covers all the spheres of activities of both the Centre and the States and that as a result, it has practically superseded the Federal Constitution so far as the States are concerned, and that the supersession is not by law but by agreement and consent and that what we now have is really totalitarianism[	Santhanam, Union State Relations. page 47.]. In view of this criticism, it becomes necessary to examine the true status and the Constitutionality of the Planning Commission.

3.15. Now it cannot be doubted that there is need for comprehensive national planning. The real controversy is as to whether the Planning Commission and its instrument, the National Development Council, are in their constitution and working, in accordance with the letter and with the real intention of the Constitution. To begin with, there is the outstanding fact that the Planning Commission is not a statutory body. There is no provision for it in the Constitution nor is there a law of Parliament constituting it. As to the competence of the Union Legislature to enact such a law reference might be made to entry 20 in List III in Schedule 7 of the Constitution, "Social and economic planning." So Parliament can enact a law on Planning under this entry. But it has been suggested as against this that in the debates in the Constituent Assembly, the subject of planning did not figure prominently. But that is immaterial if the language of entry 20 is of sufficient amplitude to comprehend it. If entry 20 does not cover it, then the matter will fall under the residual entry 97 in List I. Such a legislation therefore would be intra vires. But then, it is said that no law has in fact been enacted constituting a Planning Commission and defining its powers. That again, does not affect the validity of the commission because, the executive power of a Government is co-extensive with its legislative power. That is enacted in Article 73 which is as follows:

" 73. (1) Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, the executive power of the Union shall extend(a) to the matters with respect to which Parliament has power to make laws ; and

Provided that the executive power referred to in sub clause (a) shall not, save as expressly provided in this Constitution or in any law made by Parliament, extend in any State . . . . . to matters with respect to which the Legislature of the State has also power to make laws.''

There is a corresponding provision so far as the States are concerned in Article 162.

3.16 In Ram Jawaya Kapur v. State of Punjab[	1955 (2) SCR 225: 1955 SCJ 504: AIR 1955 SC 549.] the Punjab Government had, in pursuance of the policy of nationalisation of text-books, issued notifications, which had the effect of putting an end to the business of printing, publishing and selling text-books by the citizens. The validity of these notifications was challenged inter alia on the ground that there was no law authorising the Government to enter into the domain of trade. Overruling this contention, it was held by the Supreme Court on a comparison of Articles 73 and 162 that the executive power of the State extended to all matters with respect to which the legislature could enact laws and that accordingly the State could enter into trade without a specific legislation authorising it to do so, and that further the exercise of this power as regards matters in the Concurrent List was subject to the Proviso. On the principles laid down in the above decision it is necessary to consider whether having regard to its functions, the Planning Commission could have been validly constituted by a law of the Union Legislature.

3.17. The development plans of the Planning Commission cover, as already stated, a wide range of subjects, some of which fall under Lists I and III, and others under List II of the 7th Schedule to the Constitution. No question arises as regards the former. The Union has legislative competence over them and so can establish a commission to work on them. But as regards matters falling within List II, it is only the States Legislature that has exclusive competence over them and it is only the State Government that can set up a commission to deal with them. And if we examine the reports of the Planning Commission, it will be seen that a large number of their recommendations relate to matters in List II. The question suggests itself whether it would not be unconstitutional for the Union Legislature to enact a law with reference to the matters in List II, and whether therefore on the reasoning of the decision in Ram Jawaya Kapur's case, the Planning Commission is itself not to be an unconstitutional body. The answer to this is that the Commission is purely an advisory body. It has no executive powers ; to use a familiar expression, it has no teeth. The fact that in general the Government treats the recommendations of the Commission as binding does not alter the position. Now there can be no legal objection to the Union Government constituting a Commission to advise it on development schemes relating to the matters in the State List, as it gives grants and loans to the States for the execution of those schemes. That is all the function of the Planning Commission and there can be no question of its constitutionality. The position is a fortiori the same with reference to topics in List III, over which Parliament has legislative competence but executive action with reference thereto is subject to the proviso in Article 73.

3.18. Next, the recommendations of the Planning Commission come to the Union Government for implementation. The Union Government cannot itself carry them out in so far as they are matters within the exclusive competence of the States. What it does is to ask the State'Governments to take up the undertakings and finance them. That it does by making grants under Article 282 and by advancing loans under Article 293.

3.19. Article 282 runs as follows:

" 282. The Union or a State may make any grants for any public purpose, notwithstanding that the purpose is not one with respect to which Parliament or the Legislature of the State, as the case may be, may make laws."

Under this Article, the Union Government gives to the States what are called 'matching grants'. That is to say, the expenditure on the undertaking would be shared by the State and the Union, generally equally, but sometimes the Union Government gives more.

3.20. Then Article 293 which provides for the grant of loans is as follows :

" 293. (1) Subject to the provisions of this Article, the executive power of a State extends to borrowing within the territory of India upon the security of the Consolidated Fund of the State within such limits, if any, as may from time to time be fixed by the Legislature of such State by law and to the giving of guarantees within such limits, if any, as may be so fixed.

(2) The Government of India may, subject to such conditions as may be laid down by or under any law made by Parliament, make loans to any State or, so long as any limits fixed under Article 292 are not exceeded, give guarantees in respect of loans raised by any State, and any sums required for the purpose of making such loans shall be charged on the Consolidated Fund of India.

(3) A State may not without the consent of the Government of India raise any loan if there is still outstanding any part of a loan which has been made to the State by the Government of India or by its predecessor Government, or in respect of which a guarantee has been given by the Government of India or by its predecessor Government.

(4) A consent under clause (3) may be granted subject to such conditions, if any, as the Government of India may think fit to impose."

On the face of the Article, there is no necessary connection between the loans to be granted by the Union Government, and the carrying out of the recommendations of the Planning Commission by the States. But in practice, they were closely linked. " Here again", says Shri Santhanam, " these loans were earmarked for specific purposes. Either the States had to accept and carry out their purposes or to lose the loans. Whether the States liked a particular project or,not, they had to submit. If they did not want the project, they had to forgo the loan. It was not open to them to say that they did not want multipurpose schools or basic schools ; and they would like to spend it on buildings for general education or women's education or anything else. They would not get the grant for education or loan for buildings and so, practically, there was a rigid connection even between the loans and grants."[	Union State Relations in India, page 55.]

3.21. The scheme of financing by the Union Government of undertakings recommended by the Planning Commission has come in for severe criticism. It has been stated that it is legally unauthorised and unconstitutional and that further it is repugnant to the principles of federation and that it has reduced the States to a subordinate position. These criticisms will have now to be examined.

3.22. On the question of the legality of the grants, it is not, and it cannot be, urged that it is unconstitutional for the Union Government to make grants to the States. In America, the Federal Government does make subventions to the States when they are in need of assistance. The only question therefore is whether the grants are made under the authority of law. And that again turns on the interpretation of Article 282. The Union Government has acted on the view that that Article would be applicable if there is a public purpose and indisputably the recommendations of the Planning Commission are with respect to matters of public and national importance. The article expressly provides that a grant can be made even though the government has no legislative competence over the matter. Thus the grant is positively within the purview of the section, and negatively not prohibited by it. Therefore it is legal. That is the reasoning on which the present practice is sustained, and it proceeds on a literal interpretation of Article 282.

3.23. The argument against it is based on the principle of harmonious construction of the provisions of a Statute. Article 275 which has already been referred to provides for grants in aid being given to the States and prescribes the procedure to be followed before the grants are made. It provides that "after a finance Commission has been constituted no order shall be made under this clause by the President except after considering the recommendations of the finance Commission." Now what is said is that there being a special provision conferring authority on the Union Government to make grants it would be erroneous to put such a construction on the very general language of Article 282 as will override Article 275, and render nugatory all the safeguards provided in it. Why should the Constitution insist, it is asked, on the recommendation of the finance Commission being obtained before a grant is made if it can be given by the mere fiat of the Executive under Article 282? It is said that this is a residuary article and on the application of the maxim generalia specialibus non derogant, it has no application to the ground covered by Article 275.

3.24. There is considerable force in these criticisms. But it will be academic to go into the question further, as the entire scheme is working on the basis of consent of the States. The Chief Ministers of the States are members of the National Development Council and the principle of "matching grants" on which distributions are made under Article 282 is based on the consent of both the Union and the States. We must therefore take it that there is in this scheme nothing which is unconstitutional, in the sense that it is hit by any provision of the Constitution.

3.25. Then there is the criticism that the system of making grants by the Union Government to the States for the purpose of implementing the recommendations of the Planning Commission is repugnant to the true principles of federation under the Constitution and that it has reduced the States to a position of subordination to the Union Government. In other words the charge is that a Constitution which is federal in its structure has become unitary in its working. Now what are the facts? We start with this that the Constitution has allotted in List II certain topics exclusively to the States. In doing so, its obvious intention is to confer on them exclusive authority to evolve and carry out all undertakings in relation to those topics with such aid and advice as the Union Government might give. What has happened? By the fiat of the Union executive, a Planning Commission has been constituted with authority to propose plans with respect to all matters, including those mentioned in List II and its effect is to wrest the initiative from the States and vest it in the Union Government of which the Planning Commission is a limb. In other words, the plans are evolved by the Union Government, and they are carried out by the State Government. This is a signal example of a federal constitution being worked on unitarian lines.

3.26. But then, it is said, in answer to this, that as the Chief Ministers of the States are members of the National Development Council, which settles the plans, they would all be heard on these proposals and that the decisions taken are virtually their decisions. It is further stated that in the implementation of the recommendations of the Planning Commission, "matching grants" are given by the Union, and they are taken and utilised by the State Governments for the several projects and that accordingly there can be no infringing of the rights of the State with respect to the topics in List II. It is said as against this that having regard to the constitution of the National Development Council, the role of the Chief Ministers is purely advisory and that it is the Union Government that has an effective voice in the matters. As for the participation by the States in the implementation of the recommendations of the Planning Commission, it is said that much significance could not be attached to it as in view of the financial position of most of the States, they cannot afford to lose the massive aid given by the Union Government and that really they have no choice in the matter. It is unnecessary to discuss what value should be attached to the representation of the States in the National Development Council, or to their acceptance of "matching grants" from the Union Government, as they have a bearing only on the question of the constitutionality of the Planning Commission and not on the question Whether the scheme is in its working unitary, which is what we are considering.

3.27. On the merits of the present scheme also there is difference of opinion. One view is that there is paramount necessity to plan our economic development on modern and scientific lines and on a gigantic scale so that India might easily become self-sufficient, that the plans must be on a national basis and that it is only a machinery such as the Planning Commission and the National Development Council that can effectively do it. The other view is that while economic development on a large scale is essential, its planning should be left to be done some by the Union and some by the States. Moreover, every state, it is said, has its own peculiar conditions, needs and potentialities, and plans appropriate to them could be settled only on the State level while plans of an All India character should be left to the Union. It is further stated that List I is framed on the principle that all matters of national importance should be included therein that accordingly the topics mentioned there in are of sufficient amplitude to include all undertakings of national importance and that the Union Government can appropriately concentrate its activities on them. It will be seen from an examination of the rival views set out above that while the approach of the one to the problem is unitarian, that of the other is federal.

3.28. It is not within the scope of this lecture to suggest ways and means of reconciling the needs of the nation as a whole with the powers of the State under a federal Constitution. It can only be said that they must both be well-balanced. That is a difficult problem which all countries with a federal Constitution have to tackle. In America, President Roosevelt had to encounter widespread opposition to his New Deal legislation whose object was to give relief to the people who had been hit hard by an economic blizzard which had paralysed the entire nation. In Australia, the Labour Government initiated a comprehensive Bill in 1942 for economic planning but that failed for want of the necessary majority in referendum. That was also the fate of a Bill for regulation of marketing introduced in 1946. The position is clearly stated by Mr. Wade in his introduction to Dicey's Law of the Constitution:

"It may then be argued that the federal State Constitutes a bulwark against the dictatorship, and in particular dictatorship of the Fascist character. In every federal State there exists a dual bureaucracy, but it is a bureaucracy which is controlled by the constitution. One part cannot enlarge its powers at the expense of the other. Judicial interpretation may swing the balance in favour of Federal or State government, but a drastic increase of power one way or the other is unattainable. On the other hand, it may be difficult in such circumstances to achieve that new conception of liberty which is regarded as essential in modern civilisation. For liberty today involves the ordering of social and economic conditions by governmental authority, even in those countries where political, if not economic equality of its citizens has been attained. Without expansion of that authority which the United States as well as the Dominion of Canada and the Commonwealth of Australia have so far found more difficult to achieve than has a unitary State like the United Kingdom, there is inevitably a risk that the constitution may break down before a force which is not limited by considerations of constitutional niceties. For, the diffusion of political power, such as prevails in those great English speaking communities, seems to call insistently for the equalising of economic conditions, such as has proceeded gradually in Great Britain during the past thirty years and more."

3.29. In the backward condition of the Industries at the time of the Independence of India, a system of planning based on unitary principles was certainly necessary. It is really in the nature of an emergency measure. It is well known that even in America and in Australia strong centralising tendencies have begun to assert themselves as a result of the war conditions. The vice of it is that measures taken during times of emergency continue to live on even after normal conditions are restored and tend to become permanent. It is impossible now to say whether the economic emergency has passed and when it will be possible to go over to normal working of the Constitution. But the ultimate goal must be for the Union and the State Governments each to set up a statutory Commission with authority to frame plans with reference to the matters within their respective jurisdiction under the Constitution and when the States apply to the Union Government for grants in aid, for carrying out their plans, then for the Union Government to authorise the Union Planning Commission to scrutinize them and to make their recommendation and on that to make grants under Article 275 of the Constitution. To sum up, the current scheme of recommendation of plans by the Planning Commission and its implementation by the Union Government is, as regards matters falling within List II, not unconstitutional but it is in keeping more with a unitary than a federal Government.

3.30. The Supreme Court of India, has held that 'India is not a federal State in the traditional sense of the term. There can be no doubt as to the fact, and this is of utmost significance for purposes at hand, that in the context of India, the principle of federalism is not territory related. This is evident from the fact that India is not a true federation formed by agreement between various States and territorially it is open to the Central Government under Article 3 of the Constitution, not only to change the boundaries, but even to extinguish a State.'[	Kuldip Nayar v. Union of India, (2006) 7 SCC 1.]

3.31. It has also observed that the Constitution of India is flexible in both unitary and federal directions. It was observed:

'If then our Constitution creates a Central Government which is 'amphibian', in the sense that it can move either on the federal or unitary plane, according to the needs of the situation and circumstances of a case, the question which we are driven back to consider is whether an assessment of the 'situation' in which the Union Government should move either on the federal or unitary plane are matters for the Union Government itself or for this Court to consider and determine.'[	Kuldeep Nayar ibid.]

3.31. In regard to the relationship of Central Government with the States the Supreme Court has observed:

"The Preamble of our Constitution shows that the people of India had resolved to constitute India into a Sovereign Secular Democratic Republic and promised to secure to all its citizens Justice, Liberty and Equality and to promote among them all Fraternity assuring the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the Nation. In the people of India, therefore, vests the legal sovereignty while the political sovereignty is distributed between the Union and the States. Article 73 extends the executive power of the Union to matters with respect to which Parliament has power to make laws and to the exercise of such rights, authority and jurisdiction as are exercisable by the Government of India by virtue of any treaty or agreement. The executive power which is made co-extensive with Parliament's power to make laws shall not, save as expressly provided by the Constitution or in any law made by Parliament, extend in any State to matters with respect to which the Legislature of the State also has power to make laws. Article 162 stipulates that the executive power of a State shall extend to matters with respect to which the Legislature of the State has power to make laws provided that in any matter with respect to which the Legislature of a State and Parliament have power to make laws, the executive power of the State shall be subject to, and limited by, the executive power expressly conferred by the Constitution or by any law made by Parliament upon the Union or authorities thereof. It may also be noticed that the executive power of every State must be so exercised as not to impede or prejudice the exercise of the executive power by the Union. The executive power of the Union also extends to giving such directions to a State as may appear to the Government of India to be necessary for those purposes and as to the construction, maintenance of means of communication declared to be of national or military importance and for protection of railways. The States have to depend largely on financial assistance from the Union. Under the scheme of Articles 268 to 273, States are in Certain cases allowed to collect and retain duties imposed by the Union; in other cases taxes levied and collected by the Union are assigned to the States and in yet other cases taxes levied and collected by the Union are shared with States. Article 275 also provides for the giving of grants by the Union to certain States. There is, therefore, no doubt that States depend for financial assistance upon the Union since their power to raise resources is limited. As economic planning is a concurrent subject, every major project must receive the sanction of the Central Government for its financial assistance since discretionary power under Article 282 to make grants for public purposes is vested in the Union or a State, notwithstanding that the purpose is one in respect to which Parliament or State Legislature can make laws. It is only after a project is finally sanctioned by the Central Government that the State Government can execute the same which demonstrates the control that the Union can exercise even in regard to a matter on which the State can legislate. In addition to these controls Article 368 confers powers on Parliament to amend the Constitution, albeit by a specified majority. The power extends to amending matters pertaining to the executive as well as legislative powers of the States if the amendments are ratified by the legislatures of not less than one-half of the States. This provision empowers Parliament to so amend the Constitution as to curtail the powers of the States. A strong Central Government may not find it difficult to secure the requisite majority as well as ratification by one half of the legislatures if one goes by past experience. These limitations taken together indicate that the Constitution of India cannot be said to be truly federal in character as understood by lawyers in the United States of America."[	Per Ratnaval Pandian J in S.R. Bommai vs Union of India]

3.32. On 23.12.1993, the then Prime Minister announced the MPLAD Scheme. This scheme was formulated for enabling the Members of Parliament to identify small works of capital nature based on locally felt needs in their constituencies. The objective, as seen from the guidelines of the Scheme, is to enable the Members of Parliament to recommend works of developmental nature with emphasis on the creation of durable community assets based on the locally felt needs to be taken up in their Constituencies. The guidelines prescribe that right from inception of the Scheme, durable assets of national priorities viz., drinking water, primary education, public health, sanitation and roads etc. are being created.

3.33. This scheme was challenged before Supreme Court claiming that it was contrary to the 73rd and 74th Amendments to the Constitution of India, under which, the entire area of local self-government has been entrusted to Panchayats under Article 243G and to the Municipalities under Articles 243W, 243ZD and 243ZE read with Schedule- XII of the Constitution. By virtue of the said Amendments, the decision making power in regard to development rests with Panchayats and Municipalities, however, under this Scheme, the works are being given to individual Mps.

3.34. In respect of above the Supreme Court of India has taken a view that the Constitution of India is 'quasi-federal' and has adopted the interpretations leaning in favour of the Central Government rather than the States or Local Government. It observed:

'….Owing to the quasi-federal nature of the Constitution and the specific wording of Article 282, both the Union and the State have the power to make grants for a purpose irrespective of whether the subject matter of the purpose falls in the Seventh Schedule provided that the purpose is 'public purpose' within the meaning of the Constitution....................Panchayat Raj Institutions, Municipal as well as local bodies have also not been denuded of their role or jurisdiction by the Scheme as due place has been accorded to them by the guidelines, in the implementation of the Scheme..... '

3.35. Thus the final words, as spoken by Supreme Court itself truly conclude the debate:

'A conspectus of the provisions of our Constitution will indicate that, whatever appearances of a federal structure our constitution may have, its operations are certainly, judged both by the contents of power which a number of its provisions carry with them and the use that has been made of them, more unitary than federal.........In a sense, therefore, the Indian union is federal. But, the extent of federalism in it is largely watered down by the needs of progress and development of a country which has to be nationally integrated, politically and economically coordinated, and socially, intellectually and spiritually up-lifted. In such a system, the States cannot stand in the way of legitimate and comprehensively planned development of the country in the manner directed by the Central Government. The question of legitimacy of particular actions of the Central Government taking us in particular directions can often be tested and determined only by the verdicts of the people at appropriate times rater than by decisions of Courts. For this reasons, they become, properly speaking, matters for political debates rather than for legal discussion. If the special needs of our country, to have political coherence, national integration, and planned economic development of all parts of the country, so as to build a welfare State where "justice, social, economic and political" are to prevail and rapid strides are to be taken towards fulfilling the of her noble aspirations, set out in the Preamble, strong central directions seems inevitable. It is the country's need. That, at any rate, seems to be the basic assumption behind a number of our Constitutional provisions.'[	Per Beg C.J. In State of Rajsthan v. Union of India, 1978 (1) SCR 1 at p. 38]

Therefore any attempt to put Constitution of India in a straight jacket of ideologies or theories or doctrines of Federal or Unitary Government would be futile.
