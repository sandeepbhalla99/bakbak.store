# Preface to First Edition

[ Abridged from longer version.]

THE Constitution of India is one of the latest and easily the most elaborate among Federal Constitutions. The evolution of this form of Government constitutes one of the most fascinating chapters in the history of Political Science. In its present form, Federalism came into existence in America. People belonging to different nationalities, speaking different languages and subscribing to different religions had established several colonies in America, and at the relevant point of time they were all subject to the sovereignty of Great Britain. Disputes then arose between these colonies and Great Britain leading to a war and resulting eventually in the recognition of the independence of the former. It was at this stage that statesmen and politicians with a broad outlook conceived of a form of Government, in which each State would be sovereign in respect of all matters concerning its internal administration but would be subject to a Central Government in certain matters of common interest such as defence. This eventually resulted in the formation of the Union called the United States of America which at that time consisted of 13 States. The powers of the Union and the obligations of the States were all enumerated in a document called the Bill of Rights. Thus the Federal form of Government had its birth in political exigencies. It may be added that it tended to be strengthened by economic ties. The American Constitution has remained the model of a Federal form of Government and has been adopted in several countries with suitable modification.

Under the Government of India Act, 1935, a form of Federal Government was established in British India. With the attainment of independence, the position had to be reviewed and that was done by the Constituent Assembly in its sittings from 1947 to 1949. Taking the Act of 1935 as the basis, it suitably modified and enlarged its provisions. Having regard to the eminence of the Jurists who took part in the framing of the Constitution, it can safely be asserted that it is one of the most carefully drafted Constitutional documents, and that is proved by the fact that it has worked fairly well in practice. In the first lecture I have traced the evolution of the Constitution in some detail, and in the second I have, on a review of the provisions bearing on the question, pointed out that the Constitution is in substance Federalistic.

Finally, it cannot be overemphasized that orderly progress of a society is possible only when there is respect for law, and obedience to it. The Constitution is the Supreme Law of India, and with its Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles provides for the development of this country on principles of Justice, Liberty and Equality. It is our duty to develop reverence for it and to say and do nothing which will impair its prestige.

T.L. Venkatarama Aiyar

Retd. Judge Supreme Court of India
